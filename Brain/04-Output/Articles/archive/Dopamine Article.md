## What is it?

Dopamine is a chemical with a quite straightforward formula (which I plan to tattoo on my shoulder, if I ever get brave enough for this). It is released in particular areas of the brain, and it influences neurons in multiple different areas of the brain. Some call it a neuro-transmitter (narrow effects, used only to transmit signals between neurons), and others neuro-modulator (wider impact on neurons across the brain). 

In terms of its effects, there are so many of them that I struggle to even list them - every last one requires clarification. But think of it this way - everything you do beyond your skin and everything you feel that makes you motivated to do it is directly driven by Dopamine. Movement itself is impossible without Dopamine stimulation. 

It is in the core of making decisions and of being motivated. It dictates the level of motivation, desire and willingness to go through effort. 

It directs us to look for, want and strive for things outside ourselves. It forces us to look for novelty, explore, achieve. 

Put it another way - if you did not have Dopamine for whatever reason - you would not have motivation to even move, let alone go through the effort of achieving anything. You would not even reach for food, and could die starving. Even if the food was right in front of you. That is exactly what happened in the experiment with genetically altered mice, who did not want to come a few inches to get food and were dying starving. But if you put food in their mouth they would eat and enjoy it. 

To summarize, Dopamine is the source of motivation for basically anything outside ourselves, and it creates a feeling that life is about to get better. Right now, right after the next thing you want‚Ä¶

The most important misconception about Dopamine is that it is considered a molecule of pleasure. This is not exactly the case at all, this is a molecule of anticipation, craving, drive, motivation, excitement - sometimes those things can be experienced as pleasure. It will make us think we will like something when we get it, but it nothing to do with whether we are going to actually like it. That‚Äôs why it is such a normal thing to be super excited about getting something, and then be somewhat dissapointed after getting it. More about it below.

Roughly 1 in 20 million cells in the brain product Dopamine, but they have disproportionatly large impact on the brain.

This circuit evolved to give us motivation to get food, sex and win competitions. It is activated by something attractive from the evolutionary or life-sustaining standpoint. It is activated no matter if you are hungry. The sensation is not a Wanting, but rather a reaction to something we encounter. 

The second crucial circuit is a [[Dopamine Control (Meso-cortical) [[Circuit]] Circuit]], which goes through Pre-frontal cortex - the part of the brain that does all the rational thinking, control and explaining. Dopamine goes to both circuit simultaneously - [[  Desire Circuit (  Mesolimbic [[Pathway)]]]] and [[Dopamine Control (Meso-cortical) [[Circuit]] Circuit]] (Tenacity circuit). The first one creates the desire to get something, and the second one allows us to make a choice and plan for getting it. 

There‚Äôs a baseline level of [[Dopamine]] that is always circulating your brain, which is necessary for you to do ‚Ä¶ really anything, even move. And then there are spikes of Dopamine that make us feel exited and motivated to do something. 

Now in a super simple way, imagine there‚Äôs a small jar of Dopamine. It‚Äôs dripping a bit regularly to give you this baseline Dopamine. Then when your brain identifies something exciting (source of food, potential mate), this jar ejects peak level of dopamine into two tubes. One tube goes to the area of them brain that makes you want this thing soooo much that you are ready to do anything, and the other tube goes to the area that plans for getting it and can say NO to this potentially stupid idea. 

This jar analogy is useful, because one important thing to keep in mind is that after the spike of dopamine, there‚Äôs less of it in the jar, and so the baseline of it is dropped. And when the baseline of Dopamine drops, we tend to feel psychological pain and distress. 

After the peak the level of dopamine returns to Lower level than the baseline before the peak. Extent to which it goes under is proportional to the height of the peak. Eventually baseline will return to normal, though. 

Experiencing the same things over and over again drops the peaks levels - we develop tolerance. But different activities cause spikes of dopamine. And so peaks can come from a ton of activities, and if they are repeated one after another - the baseline of progressively dropping, which makes it harder and harder to experience excitement and ‚Äúwanting‚Äù about really anything. 

Long-term consumption of high-dopamine substances leads to Dopamine Deficit state. Dopamine runs out, as well as dopamine sensitivity decreased - and nothing feels good anymore. This is called [[Anhedonia]]  - inability to enjoy pleasure of any kind. 

The level of motivation and excitement is relative to the baseline level, meaning that how much you are motivated is basically a difference between the hight of the peak and the level of baseline. 

Level of motivation under ‚Äúnormal‚Äù level or baseline Dopamine depends on the level of Dopamine before this moment, on previous peaks. This is due to the fact that Dopamine neurons tend to develop a tolerance to high levels of Dopamine.

### Reward-Prediction Error [[Brain/Reward-Prediction Error (RPE)]]

This part is my favorite, because it speaks about probably one of the most important mechanisms in the brain that directly facilitates learning and creating behaviors. Basically, if you do something from time to time, e.g. you like to each an avocado toast in the morning, or snore a line of cocaine before going to bed - both of those behaviors are created by this mechanism. 

This is called Cue-Dependent learning, Pavlovian conditioning or Reward-Prediction Error. Different names - same approach for shaping any behavior - harmful or otherwise. 

Whenever you wonder around and find something you like (food, drug, behavior), then you try it and like it, this triggers the release of Dopamine. This is an unexpected reward. This spike stimulates learning and forcing you to remember what you have done or seen right before this this stimulus that brought you so much pleasure. This way, the Cues (hints, triggers) are formed are memorized. 

Next time you run into this Cue, it creates a spike of Dopamine and excitement about what this Cue is associated with. Cue itself can be  anything in the environment or people around you. This cue creates a small anticipatory release of Dopamine, which motivates you to pursue the reward. 

You go for the reward, and if you get it, and it meets or exceeds your expectation - you get an even bigger spike of Dopamine. This spike stimulates the learning process, basically making you remember that the sequence Cue ‚Üí Reward feels good, and that you should do that next time. 

If, however, the reward does not meet your expectations (you don‚Äôt like it) - Dopamine drops below the baseline, which feels awful. It triggers the opposite process, teaching your brain that this sequence of Cue ‚Üí Reward is not as good for you, and next time you are less likely to execute this behavior. 

This simple algorithm creates a chain of behaviors and habits, that follow basic logic of Cue ‚Üí Craving ‚Üí Reward ‚Üí Reinforcement.

It‚Äôs the same cycle discussed in dozens of books about Habits. 

The only way that really works for me in terms of changing the behavior is changing my environment, and by doing that, removing the Cues that triggered behaviors. In most cases, we don‚Äôt realize what are the Cues that make us execute on some behaviors, and so we can‚Äôt fix a harmful behavior. 

Important learning from this section is that Dopamine spike (Reward) or drop (punishment) are instruments of learning. This is how we get to know the world. Unexpected reward or punishment is Learning. To support this idea, research suggests that Dopamine spikes triggers neuroplasticity - brain forming new connections between neurons. 

There‚Äôs a physical process of forming Tolerance to any stimulus - behaviors or drugs. As I understand, there are two ways in which it happens. 

1. Dopamine receptors become less receptive to Dopamine spikes, so even the same amount of Dopamine does not make us feel as good
2. Since Dopamine is spiking as a response to Unexpected reward, or reward exceeding expectations - with repeated behaviors, the spike of dopamine becomes smaller and smaller. Unless, of course, we can come up with something new every time. Hence - a search for novelty.

### [[Addiction]]s

It happened for a reason that Dopamine is often mentioned in the same sentence as addiction - mechanisms that regulate Dopamine cause the addictions, and its both - blessing and the curse. 

[[Addiction]] is an irresistible (and often harmful) behavior that you can‚Äôt control. It tends to ruin lives and makes us, humans, feel captive, without control over our lives. It is a horrible thing, yet it is just a natural product of the mechanism that makes us human. 

In the previous sections we talked about the Reward-Prediction Error. This is in the core of forming addictions. To remind you, it gives you a spike of dopamine when you receive an unexpected stimulation that brings you pleasure, and then associates the Cues of this stimulation to make you repeat it again. 

There are a few issues with this mechanism:

1. Using the same stimulus creates tolerance to it, so it requires more and more of this stimulus (more in volume or frequency) to get to the same level of pleasure. This is actually good, because for moderate amounts of stimulation (like food and sex) it makes us stop doing it so often - until the tolerance eases down and we can enjoy it again. But for for hard drugs. 
2. If this moderate stimulation is done often (causes frequent spikes of Dopamine) - it depletes the baseline of Dopamine. When Dopamine baseline is depleted - we are in psychological and sometimes physical pain. And the only way to resolve this pain becomes to find new spikes of Dopamine. And we often turn to the thing (substance of behavior) that generates the most of it for us. And then the cycle repeats itself. This part is crucial, because gradually any kind of addiction makes us repeat the behavior not because we WANT to do it now, but because we are in PAIN without this stimulus. This is how ‚ÄúI take it because it makes me feel good‚Äù turns into ‚ÄúI literally can‚Äôt function without it‚Äù.
3. If the dopamine is stimulated by highly stimulating drug - the baseline drops way below the baseline, and it takes too much time and effort to recover. Also, after experiencing this high level of Dopamine, brain develops tolerance to less potents stimulus, and everything else except for the drug becomes way less desirable. 

This process on its own can create addictions to things like food or sex - those that bring you moderate spike in Dopamine. 

**When you repeatedly do something your enjoy (what brings you Dopamine spikes) - threshold for enjoyment goes up and up, so it is harder and harder to experience the same level of pleasure. Gradually these spikes are lower, but ‚Äúpain‚Äù spikes are created that makes us want to keep doing this thing. So instead of pleasure there‚Äôs pain that forces us to do things during the addiction.**  

Drug usage (cocaine, met) depletes one of the neural pathways for dopamine. Same pathway as for reward, motivation and craving.
