# Speed beats perfection - decision velocity over decision quality

**Source:** Eugene Kurogo LinkedIn - Good & Quick Over Best & Slow 3dcc60013b964c41af3bd37305135ae8
**Date Captured:** 2025-10-24
**Type:** Experience Wisdom / Contrarian View

## Insight

The speed of your decisions matters far more than their quality. In startups, waiting for the "perfect" decision leads to analysis paralysisâ€”by the time you decide, someone else will have moved ahead. The best way forward is to run fast experiments, learn, and adapt. Aim for momentum, not perfection.

## Context

Eugene describes his expectation when joining YayPay in 2016: "I literally assumed the success of a startup relied on making the best possible decisions. Spoiler: I was so wrong."

**Framework:**
1. Make good-enough decisions quickly (speed over perfection, then iterate)
2. Fall in love with experiments (trial-and-error is your best friend)
3. Act fast (first-mover advantage in gaining traction and raising funds)

**Core principle:** "Speed ALWAYS beats perfection"

## Connection to Existing Knowledge

Integrates with:
- **Decision-making under uncertainty** - perfect information is impossible, speed matters
- **Optionality** - fast decisions preserve options, slow decisions foreclose them
- **OODA Loop** (Boyd) - observe-orient-decide-act cycle speed as competitive advantage
- **Exploration vs. Exploitation** - fast experiments enable more exploration
- **Resulting fallacy** - judging decisions by outcomes vs. process (speed is process metric)
- **Kelly Criterion** - bet sizing matters more than bet perfection

## Apparent Tension

This creates interesting tension with:
- **Systematic decision-making** frameworks (which Eugene also values)
- **Process optimization** for judgment quality
- Question: When does speed sacrifice too much quality? Where's the threshold?

Possible resolution: Speed for **verifiable** decisions (can test quickly), slower for **non-verifiable** decisions (can't reverse easily). But even then, Eugene suggests speed wins.
